Project context & motivation

About the IONO-DIET project.

IONO-DIET is an interdisciplinary project that connects natural hazards and space sciences. Its main goal is to build the first ionosphere-based system able to detect natural hazards in near-real time and to infer key source parameters directly from the ionosphere.

Priority is given to submarine earthquakes and tsunamis, due to their strong societal impact and the need for rapid and reliable early warning.

Context and positioning

Why earthquakes, tsunamis and the ionosphere?

Natural hazards and societal impact

Earthquakes and tsunamis are among the most destructive natural hazards on Earth. Every year, they cause substantial human and economic losses. While earthquakes cannot be predicted, their impact can be mitigated by improving existing tsunami warning systems and rapid hazard assessment. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Why use the ionosphere?

Strong earthquakes and tsunamis launch acousticâ€“gravity waves that propagate upward and perturb the ionosphere within minutes. These co-seismic and co-tsunamic ionospheric disturbances can be detected with GNSS-based TEC, magnetic measurements and airglow observations, providing an independent view of the event from above. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

The ionosphere therefore offers an additional time window and a complementary physical observable that can help refine estimates of earthquake magnitude, uplift and tsunami potential, especially for the largest and most complex events.

IONO-DIET is positioned at the interface between solid Earth geophysics, atmospheric and ionospheric physics, GNSS technology and tsunami science, aiming to unlock the full potential of ionospheric observations for natural hazard early warning.

Main goal of IONO-DIET

From ionospheric signatures to source parameters

An ionosphere-based NRT detection system

The overarching goal of IONO-DIET is to develop an ionosphere-based system that:

1) detects natural hazards in near-real time, and
2) determines associated source parameters from the ionosphere itself, including earthquake magnitude, crustal uplift, source extent and tsunami wave heights, wherever possible. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
